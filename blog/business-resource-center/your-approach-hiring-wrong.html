Is Your Approach to Hiring Wrong?

Kelton Reid
January 14, 2021

We understand the unique challenges that today’s businesses face when it comes to hiring top tech talent. The demand for experienced IT professionals is at an all-time high, while the pool of qualified candidates actively seeking new roles remains limited. According to a recent report from Gartner, only 29% of tech professionals are actively job-hunting, yet 90% are open to better opportunities if approached strategically.

82% of companies have plans to increase investments in emerging technologies like AI, cloud infrastructure, and cybersecurity. This shift places tech talent acquisition at the forefront of executive concerns. CEOs and CIOs alike are grappling with questions such as:

  • How do we attract candidates with specialized skills in areas like machine learning and cloud architecture?
  • How can we reduce the time-to-hire without sacrificing quality?
  • What hiring approaches are proven to reduce turnover and increase productivity in high-demand roles?

Landing the right talent is not just a task for HR anymore; it's a mission-critical initiative tied directly to innovation and market competitiveness.

That hasn’t changed, in fact it’s only gotten more dire. And it’s no secret that the hiring industrial complex has become a drain on resources with little proven benefit.

Why the #FutureofWork is about more than just filling your funnel with warm bodies

The real challenge lies in securing—and keeping—top talent. 40% of U.S. companies now outsource their hiring process, often relying on recruitment outsourcing providers who, in turn, subcontract recruiters to scour LinkedIn, social media sites, and talent platforms for passive candidates.

This flood of outreach has created candidate fatigue, particularly among highly skilled tech professionals. Many employed IT pros report being contacted up to 30 times a day, with recruiters offering roles that often feel transactional or misaligned with their career goals. The hiring market has become a battleground where companies engage in bidding wars for talent, driving up salaries and churn rates in the process.

But here’s the truth: it doesn’t have to be a race to the top bidder. We’ve discussed how smaller businesses can compete by taking a different approach—one that emphasizes culture, career development, and meaningful work.

Is your hiring approach backward-looking or future proof?

It's not just about deciding which overseas vendor or recruitment software to outsource the task to. The real challenge lies in making strategic hiring decisions that go beyond technical skills and focus on culture fit and long-term retention.

At Esteemed.io, with over a decade of experience helping brands and agencies source top-tier developers, we’ve seen firsthand how this oversight can hinder growth. Many businesses fall into the trap of focusing solely on filling roles quickly without considering how new hires will integrate into the company’s culture or contribute to long-term success.

While technical expertise is crucial, building a truly A-class team requires more than just matching skills to job descriptions. Companies with a strong cultural alignment in their hiring process see 30% higher retention rates and significantly better performance outcomes (SHRM).

When you fail to prioritize culture fit, the consequences are costly:

  • Higher turnover: Poorly aligned hires often leave within the first year, leading to increased rehiring costs and project delays.
  • Decreased team morale: Misaligned hires can disrupt team dynamics and collaboration.
  • Lost productivity: New hires who don’t integrate well take longer to become productive, affecting overall business performance.

By contrast, businesses that recruit with both skills and culture in mind build resilient, high-performing teams capable of scaling with their operations.

Big data and hiring algorithms won’t save your business

It's an uncomfortable truth that many businesses are realizing too late: those passive IT hotshots—highly sought-after yet not actively job-hunting—aren’t always worth the significant investment. Despite the hype surrounding passive talent, there is no consistent evidence that they outperform active candidates. Yet, companies spend nearly $20 billion a year on human resource vendors and recruitment farms trying to attract them.

Passive hires often take longer to onboard and reach full productivity. Their delayed contributions can disrupt existing workflows, frustrating current employees who must pick up the slack in the meantime.

Additionally, the outsourcing model tends to favor quantity over quality. Many recruitment farms prioritize filling the funnel with candidates rather than ensuring a strong cultural or skill fit, which can:

  • Extend time-to-hire: Lengthy recruitment cycles can delay mission-critical projects.
  • Increase turnover: Misaligned hires who struggle to adapt to company culture often leave within a year, leading to expensive re-recruitment efforts.
  • Drain resources: Investing heavily in passive candidates who don’t perform as expected can impact long-term growth and operational efficiency.

In contrast, businesses that take a targeted hiring approach—focusing on both skill and fit—see improved retention and faster time-to-productivity. The solution lies in building talent pipelines tailored to your business needs. Instead of relying on recruitment farms, invest in direct sourcing, real connections with RPO teams, employee referrals, and ongoing engagement with potential hires.

Is your applicant-tracking software worth the headache?

The hiring landscape is rapidly evolving, and while applicant-tracking software (ATS) may seem like a convenient solution, it could actually harm your company culture if misused. Many ATS platforms promise streamlined processes, but at what cost? Can algorithms effectively measure the nuances that make a candidate the right cultural fit—such as body language, empathy, and adaptability?

While automation and data analytics can improve efficiency, they often lack critical human context in the hiring process. Algorithms are built on historical data, which may reinforce biases or fail to account for soft skills—like leadership potential, emotional intelligence, or communication style—that are vital to long-term success.

A survey by LinkedIn found that 60% of hiring managers believe that hiring tools relying solely on metrics fail to capture important candidate attributes. Additionally, 33% of recruiters report that automated systems frequently misidentify top candidates due to poorly defined filters and keyword mismatches.

Without the human touch, recruitment technology can create bottlenecks, leading to:

  • Inaccurate candidate filtering: Potential high-performers may be eliminated due to rigid algorithmic criteria.
  • Loss of personal engagement: Candidates who feel they’re being evaluated by bots instead of people are less likely to feel connected to your organization.
  • Failure to identify cultural fit: Successful hires require more than technical expertise—they need to align with your company’s mission and values.

The key to a successful hiring strategy is not eliminating automation but balancing technology with human expertise. Data-driven tools can enhance efficiency by automating administrative tasks, but human insight should guide critical decisions around sourcing, vetting, and retention.

Here’s how to combine both for maximum impact:

  1. Use AI to streamline repetitive tasks:
    Automate tasks like scheduling interviews, initial resume screening, and tracking candidate progress to save time for your HR team. However, leave the deeper candidate evaluation to experienced recruiters and hiring managers.
     
  2. Focus on human-centered vetting:
    Structured interviews conducted by trained professionals can provide the context algorithms lack. Candidates should be evaluated for not only their technical skills but also their ability to collaborate, innovate, and fit within the team dynamic.
     
  3. Leverage predictive analytics with caution:
    Predictive hiring tools can offer useful insights but should be treated as supplemental data, not the final decision-makers. For example, analytics may identify patterns in successful hires, but hiring managers should validate these findings with their own assessments.
